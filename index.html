Understanding Decoloniality

Critical reading activity based on "Decolonising Education in Africa"

Article Summary

This article explores the complex concept of decoloniality, particularly in the context of African education. It distinguishes decolonization from reverse colonialism, examines how colonialism entrenched conditions of injustice (coloniality of being, knowledge, and power), and discusses what decolonizing African education means in practice.

Read the Full Article:

üì∞ Decolonising Education in Africa: What does it mean
"To decolonize is not to erase the history of colonialism or to, in some radical way, reclaim the Africa that was, before the colonial encounter." - Article Author

Analytical Questions

1. Who is the intended audience of this article? Identify specific language, examples, or arguments that suggest who the author is addressing.

2. Based on your reading of the text, how would you define 'decoloniality' in 2-3 sentences? Be sure to incorporate key concepts from the article.

3. Analyze how the author uses the Stellenbosch University incident to develop their argument about decoloniality. What specific point is the author making through this example?

4. Examine the author's discussion of the three forms of coloniality (being, knowledge, power). How do these concepts interconnect to form a comprehensive understanding of colonial impact?

5. How does the author differentiate between different approaches to decolonization (healing vs. revenge)? What implications does this distinction have for educational transformation?

Key Tips for Your Analysis

  • Pay attention to how the author constructs definitions and arguments
  • Consider how examples like the Stellenbosch incident support broader theoretical points
  • Think about the relationship between personal identity and larger social structures
  • Reflect on how disciplinary perspectives might influence your understanding

Reflection Questions

1. How does your own background, identity, and educational experiences shape your understanding of decoloniality? Consider your positionality in relation to questions of knowledge, power, and identity discussed in the article.

2. Reflect on the concept of "coloniality of knowledge" as discussed in the article. How does this concept challenge or confirm your previous understanding of what counts as valid knowledge in academic settings?

3. The article discusses the importance of "re-humanising" education. What does this mean to you, and how might it be practically implemented in educational contexts you're familiar with?

4. How does your academic discipline approach questions of knowledge production, power, and social justice? In what ways might a decolonial perspective enhance or challenge disciplinary conventions in your field?

5. The author argues that "decolonization is not a vengeful project." What are your thoughts on this perspective? How might this approach influence efforts to create more equitable educational environments?
